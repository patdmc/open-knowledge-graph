# The Finite Thing

Tricia Duryee broke big news stories. That is how she introduced herself to me. "I break big news stories, and you can read one tomorrow on A1." I did. It was the front page of the Seattle Times. I was impressed. I stayed impressed for eighteen years.

We met in 2006. Tricia was covering the tech beat at the Seattle Times, where she drove the Tech Tracks blog — one of the paper's first forays into digital. She was tenacious, precise, and fearless. She would ask a powerful person the question they did not want to answer. Her favorite thing was when a source would say "I can't believe I'm telling you this" — and then give her the scoop. She reveled in revealing secret truth to the world.

I am the opposite. I am a software engineer who never trusted anything until I could prove it. Especially what I knew. In life I would guess casually — "that bird looks like a cardinal" — and be right, because I have good pattern recognition. But professionally I needed the proof before I would commit. She needed the question to be worth asking. Between the two of us, nothing got left unasked and nothing got left unverified.

She was a researcher, then an interrogator. I was a predictor, then a validator. Both paths arrive at verified knowledge. The traversal is opposite. I lived the escalation hierarchy. She traced the control structure. When I finally built the thing that could explain what killed her, I recognized the architecture. It was mine.

Tricia died on August 30th, 2024. She was 46. Breast cancer. HR-positive, metastatic. A mutation in a gene called RAD51C — one of the genes that fixes broken DNA. She fought it for nine years. Three kids, four cities, seven drugs, too many rounds of radiation.

This is a story about what I learned after she died. But everything I learned was already there before. I just could not name it yet.

---

## I.

When I was ten my family moved from Chicago to California. My grandparents' health was failing. There was a deal: my parents would sell the house in Chicago, and my grandfather would sell them the California house at the same price. After we sold and moved, my grandfather tried to back out. He wanted more money. My parents did not have it.

They talked about it every night at dinner. For months.

I was flying into rages at little things. Losing to my older brothers when I never had a chance in the first place — and them letting me know it. Unfairness in how things were split up. A lighthearted tease taken too hard. Finally I lost it after dinner and my dad grabbed me. His arms were iron from years of working on industrial roofs. I could not move. I could not fight. I gave up.

But I asked my parents to do one thing. Stop talking about their financial distress every night at dinner.

They did. And it was better for everyone — even them. That crisis was solved. But the systemic problem was not.

When my sister got teased in fourth grade, the response was to homeschool her for the next eight years. A permanent crisis. Compounded by my brother getting his girlfriend pregnant. The second hit. An existential warzone, and I was collateral damage. So I did what you do in that situation. You adapt. Or you fail.

They did not recognize the pattern the next time a crisis hit. Or the time after that. So we relived it over and over. And I became more and more self-sufficient.

I love my parents. They did their best. But they were not equipped. Not a choice — a capacity limit. When you are managing crisis, there is no bandwidth left for the kid who is not in crisis. I did not fuck up. I did not need help. I could take it. So I did.

My mom took us camping constantly in California. It was the best part of my childhood. Pure chaos. We could pack the car in thirty minutes, stop for groceries on the way to the mountains, figure out the campground on the fly. Sometimes we got campsites in Yosemite Valley with no reservations. Sometimes we crept into KOAs hours after dark. We were ready for any camping situation but we never planned.

I loved it. The spontaneity, the adventure, the readiness. But the same approach applied to finances and health and crisis meant everything was always from scratch. Always reacting. Never encoding. The camping worked because the stakes were low and we were tough. The dinner table did not work because financial ruin is not something you can improvise your way through.

I grew up in a family that searched from scratch every time. I spent the rest of my life building the thing that remembers.

## II.

I went to college for math. I failed out of grad school.

I was capable. I know that now — this work proves it. But I did not know how to ask for help. Every support network I had known up to that point had either been insufficient or made things harder. So I did what I always did: figure it out alone, go deep, try hard, extract everything I could from the one answer I got.

That drive — the refusal to ask the same question twice, the compulsion to look deeper with less — would later build everything. But it cost me grad school. The strength and the weakness are the same thing.

In fact, the only way I knew how to get help was to deliberately fail. Because then it was a crisis. And crisis was the only mode where help arrived in my family. The system did not respond at low severity. It only triggered at critical. So if I needed support, the problem had to break first.

I remember sitting in a professor's office, thinking I would have to pay back my grant because I was failing. He asked me if I was seeing a counselor. If I had talked to anyone. What my advisor had said. And I realized I had never even considered any of it. He was running through the standard escalation path — counselor, advisor, support system — and every node was empty. Not because I was stubborn. Because asking for help was not in my graph. The edge did not exist. No one had ever wired it. Not that I refused help. That there was help to be had had never occurred to me.

I had to learn to ask for help at work. It took years. The moment I stopped asking my boss and started asking the software engineers was the moment my career actually began. Asking the right person the right question is not weakness. It is the most efficient traversal of the graph.

## III.

After grad school I did not know what to do. I was working temp jobs.

A friend at a startup called SingleStep Technologies got me an interview. They offered me a job — thirteen dollars an hour, no benefits. I had also applied to be an engineering apprentice. I was accepted, and I had a choice. Take less pay, less security, less everything — or take the sure thing. But I knew I could do more than be an electrician. I bet on myself.

This journey quite literally started with a SingleStep.

SingleStep was an interesting company. Their technology was a GUI-based system where you connected execution blocks with arrows. You were making a DAG. It always stuck with me. I could understand that better than object-oriented design.

They lost funding and I was let go. I found a job at RealNetworks. They liked me but I had almost no technical skill. The hiring manager wanted me to write a bash script. I told him I could not do it. He gave me a chance — come back tomorrow, write what he wanted, and the job was mine.

Getting home, I had an old Windows computer my friend had given me. I had never owned my own computer before. I had to write every paper in college in the computer lab. So I installed Cygwin — a Linux interface for Windows — and got started. I never believed in all-nighters. Tired brains are dumb brains. But I had no choice. I spent the night learning enough nano and looping and conditionals to give myself a shot.

The next morning at RealNetworks, I had to write the bash script. No notes. No references. And I did. And I got the offer.

RealNetworks was a dinosaur from the 1990s, still barely chugging along. When they won a $500 million antitrust lawsuit against Microsoft, it was the first time the company had been profitable in years. The company survived on litigation, not on product. I worked there for a year.

Then SingleStep called me back. They had a full-time offer. They had to replace me with two full-time QA engineers. They did not know what they had until they let me go. I gave them another chance because they gave me my first one. A bidirectional edge. And it was the next step in my career.

When SingleStep finally closed, my boss Tom's wife worked at WhitePages. They hired three of us. Another interview. The last real interview of my career. Everything else has been connected edges. Nathan was a founder at Dwellable. He remembered I only asked once.

Manual QA is testing software by hand. Clicking through interfaces. Filing bugs. No code. No computer science degree. The QA mindset is adversarial — you assume the system is broken and you try to prove it. You decompose by probing. No encapsulation. No dependency trees. Just: here is the system, find the boundaries by walking it blind.

But I could see in 2006 that manual QA was going to die. People were writing more tests. Unit tests, integration tests — testing each logical assertion in isolation. The work I did by hand would be done by code. I needed to evolve.

At WhitePages I was a business analyst. Step up in pay. I learned the business side. I did the weekly metrics reporting for our ad impressions and presented them to leadership. I got comfortable speaking noncontroversial truth to power.

But the reporting process was ridiculous. It took all day. Copy-pasting queries and results from an Oracle UI. So I bought an Oracle book and automated the whole thing.

One Friday, I had not done the report. My boss wanted it. He was worried it would not get done in time. I sent it to him five minutes later. He did not believe me. He made me do it manually. He did not know I had already been running it side by side for weeks. It worked. I had proof. But he did not.

That was the first time I understood the gap between knowing something and someone else knowing you know it. The information existed. The connection did not.

My team became responsible for deduplicating billions of people records from many sources. Before, we literally loaded fixed-width columns from a DVD straight to the database. The old analysts had notes like: "Run this step 7 times. I don't know why but the data looks right." Backup files of configs everywhere. Impossible to know what was current.

I stopped asking my boss for advice and started asking the smart and helpful software engineers. That is where the real learning started.

## IV.

I taught myself to code. The first real lesson was functional composition — pure functions, no side effects, input-output contracts. Break the problem into the smallest units that can be independently reasoned about. Wire them together. If each function does one thing, the composition handles everything.

That is how you decompose a skill when you have no formal training. No mixins. No inheritance hierarchies to lean on. Just: does this function do one thing, and can I plug it into the next one?

A mentor at WhitePages, Nathan Kriege, taught me version control — git, getting the data pipeline code into repos. That was the first graph I ever used. A directed acyclic graph of commits. Shared state with branching, merging, history. I did not know to call it a graph. But it was one.

Then MapReduce. Big data. Distributed computation. MapReduce is a context management game. You preload maps with as much work as you can. You reduce on a single instance so you can do it in one pass. You partition well and only handle deep context in small groups. You winnow to a solvable space.

The math lesson inside MapReduce was this: the fastest way to improve your knowledge is to group observations by equivalence class first, find the deltas within each class, then run inference on the set of classes. Not on the items. On the categories. Factor before you compute.

That principle would turn out to be the whole thing. But I did not know that yet.

## IV.

Oscar was our first child.

Tricia was 23 weeks pregnant when she came back from a conference feeling strange. We went to the doctor and discovered she had a bicornuate uterus — a heart-shaped uterus, divided into two chambers. What we had thought was a cervix was actually stitches around a puncture to let menstrual blood out.

She had had surgery for this in middle school. Spent a couple of months out of school. Her parents were Christian Scientists, so it did not get treated well at first. It took months to diagnose. She was a kid. No one explained what had been done to her body.

Our perinatal doctor found the surgeon's notes on microfiche. Barely readable. Decades old. The information about what had been done to my wife existed on a piece of film in a filing cabinet somewhere, disconnected from every doctor who would ever need it.

I was dumbfounded. How did Tricia not know this? It was hard for me to accept and not feel betrayed. Not by her. By the system that had failed at every step — the delayed diagnosis, the surgical patch, the silence about what it meant for her future.

After a week in the hospital we had to decide. Every hour her risk of fatal hemorrhage increased. The uterine wall was filling up like a balloon. Oscar was perfectly healthy. But we had to choose Tricia.

He weighed 14 ounces. He fit in one hand. I felt his body turn cold.

We had just closed on a house. We signed the mortgage papers in the hospital. Our first trip to the empty house — now ours — with no child.

---

The surgeon's notes on microfiche. The Oracle report my boss did not believe. The analyst's note that said "run this step 7 times." In each case the information existed. In each case it was not connected to the person who needed it, when they needed it.

I did not see the pattern yet. I was living it.

## V.

Tricia got offered the AllThingsD job on our honeymoon in 2010. Kara Swisher's operation, the Wall Street Journal's tech division. She had earned it. Garfield High School to the University of Oregon to the Seattle Times — where she drove the Tech Tracks blog, one of the first blogger journalists — to PaidContent in 2008, to this. Every step harder than the last.

She started that fall. It was terrible for us as a family. Lots of evenings alone. She was always stressed. AllThingsD was a cancer. It had metastasized through our life.

Oscar died while she was working there and I was at WhitePages.

When we got pregnant again with Dylan, the writing was on the wall. In March, Tricia was three months pregnant and they did not renew her contract.

She tried to play it off. I knew it crushed her. She had worked so hard for that.

Kara Swisher — married to a Google executive, working 80 hours a week, always had nannies — could not see how having a child would change someone's priorities because it never changed hers. You cannot see an edge you never walked.

They did not want to pay for her to be pregnant and have a baby. That is what it was.

But Tricia would say, later, that it was a gift. She had to grieve it first. Losing that job probably saved our marriage. We got to do stuff together again. She was not always stressed.

Dylan. We implanted on the healthy side of the uterus. Tricia got a cerclage and was on bed rest for the last thirteen weeks — from week 23 on. The same boundary as Oscar. But this time we knew, and she held still to get past it.

Dylan was born at five pounds. Healthy. Home after two days. No NICU.

After Dylan, Tricia got a part-time gig at GeekWire. It felt like a big step down from AllThingsD. It was perfect. She did not care about missing work anymore. And I was getting raises.

## VI.

Around the same time I was building my career, I was coaching the University of Washington ultimate frisbee team. Nine years. 2004 to 2013. I started with the B team — two years there — then seven as the A team coach. I called them my practice children.

That is not a joke. I was a person who did not know how to ask for help, learning how to teach others to ask for it. A kid who grew up in a system that handled every problem with a crisis response, building a system that responded all the time so we could be ready when crisis really hit. The axioms, the accountability, the shared intelligence. The support structure I never had, built for twenty-year-olds on a field. Before Oscar. Before Dylan and Colin.

I failed before I succeeded.

I thought coaching was about strategy. I tried to script complex plays and expected the players to execute. But ultimate is dynamic. The disc moves, the defense shifts, the plan breaks. You cannot script a dynamic system. The players could not adapt because they did not understand the principles underneath the scripts.

I had to strip it back to axioms.

Handlers always attack upfield. You sprint to clear space. Defensive positioning follows these rules. Simple rules that drove complex behavior. Proper edge definition.

I scripted practices down to the minute. Maximized touches — no standing around, no drills where most of the movement was two players while everyone watched. Everything was a competition. I could not waste time.

But I could not make a team care more about winning than me. They had to hold each other accountable. They had to quest for it. I could build the infrastructure — the drills, the competition, the decomposed game. The drive had to come from them.

The hardest problem was turnover. Your most knowledgeable players graduate every year. The knowledge walks out with them unless you have encoded it in something durable. Not in a person. In the system. The axioms are the graph. The players are the traversal. The graph has to survive the players leaving.

And there was a lesson about context windows. You are dumb when you are tired. Your brain has less oxygen. Every axiom you have trained into muscle memory is one fewer thing competing for a context window that is shrinking every minute of the game. The defaults have to survive exhaustion. That is the whole point of compressing the known — to free up bandwidth for the novel.

## VII.

When Dylan was eight months old I started at a startup called Dwellable — a vacation rental app with a Tinder-like swipe interface. I was their first engineer hire. I managed the data pipeline, built the checkout and quoting architecture, moved our data storage to AWS, helped close potential partners. They let me do what I thought was right because I could do the work and I understood what the business needed.

We got pregnant with Colin in 2015. That summer Dwellable was acquired by HomeAway. The deal closed the day Expedia announced it was buying HomeAway — same earnings call. Moving to Austin was required. I had to be there by January 1st. Colin was born November 17th.

We never sold the Seattle house. We thought we might move back.

I had never written Java before. HomeAway's mobile backend was a Java monolith that served all the APIs for both traveler and partner applications. The surface area was equivalent to all our web applications combined. You could not even book on the app — checkout was a web view. I was responsible for it. For the first three months I was still in Seattle, working remote into a codebase and a language I had never touched, while Tricia was eight months pregnant and we were packing. I could not even get the applications to compile — my network permissions were wrong, no access to the Maven repository. Learning a language I had never written, in a codebase I could not build, from two thousand miles away. The other mobile backend engineer would just not show up to work — for weeks at a time. And things broke constantly. We connected to other applications via client libraries they published, but they shipped breaking changes without telling their clients. If you happened to be behind a version when they deployed to production, it caused an outage. Not an incident — we did not really have those yet. HomeAway was still shutting the entire site down for four hours every month to do database updates.

New city. New baby. New language. A codebase that could not compile, a coworker who did not show up, dependencies that broke without warning, and an organization that thought a monthly four-hour outage was normal.

In March 2016 — three months in — Tricia found a lump. One in six chance the lump needs a biopsy. We were hoping it was just a clogged duct. One in four the biopsy comes back bad.

About a 5% chance overall. It was 100% for us.

No network. Just making friends. New town. We probably should have moved back, but to what job? We needed my salary, my insurance, and the four-year payout to make it all worth it.

It was Stage 2A. We thought local treatment would be fine.

## VIII.

So I dug myself out the same way I always did. One edge at a time.

I built the first Swagger plugin so I could see the API contracts — what each service actually expected and returned. Teams would not accept my pull request adding it to their application, so I maintained forks of every app we depended on with the plugin integrated. For months I ran my own copies of their code just to be able to read the interfaces.

I forced the teams we depended on to verify we were on the right client version before they shipped every week. I started breaking up 2,000-line class files. Pushing shared logic into libraries that the web teams could use too. Decomposing the monolith from the inside.

The same thing I had done at WhitePages with the Oracle report. The same thing I had done with the deduplication pipeline. Walk the system blind, find the boundaries, automate the thing nobody thought could be automated.

Then we went whole hog with GraphQL. Full decomposition — not just breaking up the monolith but mapping every service directly to its business domain. I built a GraphQL business layer. A set of composable, domain-scoped JavaScript libraries — called GraphQL partials — that could be assembled into any configuration. Each partial knew its dependent packages. We used currying to build complex, multidomain interactions — each partial added one layer of context, and the composition handled the rest. The API deployed as a directed acyclic graph. An ephemeral BFF layer built on shared components that executed like a DAG.

I had a team of four including me. We rebuilt the entire business layer of VRBO's traveler applications. I coached the organization through it the same way I coached the ultimate team — define the axioms, implement the reference examples, let the teams compose their own solutions.

The checkout code we wrote is still running in production today. Basically unupdated for five years. They cannot beat the conversion of our simple system. To beat a single UI with well-factored sources of truth, handled in a single call with partial-level errors, you would have to be flawless. The replacement attempt is nine independent UIs all fetching data from a shared cache, each with its own failure modes, needing to be alerted for when to update. A distributed consensus problem nobody designed as one.

I also cut response times for search and details pages by 50% and costs by the same amount. COVID proved it was structural — traffic dropped, came back, costs never came back up. Conversion rate higher. Customers happier.

I shipped it two weeks after the promotion cycle closed. The person who got promoted instead of me left within nine months. He got the title he needed.

I got promoted six months later. And some kind of special recognition award. But the lesson was clear. People get promoted for shipping complexity. Architecture is about simplicity.

Architecture is a plan to move forward. On the surface it looks smooth and calm. Like a duck swimming across a pond. But underneath the surface its feet are churning. You do not see it because it is purpose-driven and encapsulated. Unneeded complexity is like a bug stuck on its back. Its legs are flailing. Easy to see. And when it rights itself everyone notices and the builder gets rewarded for flipping the bug back on its feet and moving forward. Meanwhile the duck is already across the pond.

---

Here is how a clinical trial works. A pharmaceutical company identifies a biological mechanism. They design a molecule that targets it. They test it in cells, then mice, then people. At each stage, they define eligibility criteria — which patients are allowed in. For a PARP inhibitor, the criteria say: you must have a mutation in one of these specific genes.

The list comes from prior studies showing that mutations in those genes make cells vulnerable to the drug. Each study tested one gene at a time. Each took years. Each cost millions. The list grew by accretion — one confirmed gene, then another, then another.

No one asked whether the list was complete. They asked whether each gene on the list was correct. Those are different questions. The first is about items. The second is about the category.

Trials are built on whats. This gene. This drug. This dose. This endpoint. The what is testable, fundable, publishable. The why — why does this gene respond, what category does it belong to, what else shares the category — is not part of the methodology. It comes later, or from someone else, or not at all.

The researcher who ran the study probably learned the why. But the community takes the what. The what travels in abstracts, citation counts, and eligibility criteria. The why stays in the researcher's head, or in the discussion section that nobody reads, and it does not compound.

## IX.

The surgeon left thin margins. Her doctor ignored her neck pain for months.

On Dylan's fourth birthday — August 2017, four months after we moved into the house we bought in Austin — we got the Stage 4 news. Another house. Another future changing shape at the exact moment we had settled into it.

The neck pain was the answer nobody checked. Over 70% of the bone in her C6 vertebra was gone. The cancer had eaten through it while her doctor was ignoring the symptom. The first surgeon would not do the kyphoplasty. Too risky. The second surgeon at the same hospital did it.

Austin in 2016 was not a major cancer center. It is building one now. Too late for us.

Every system I have ever debugged fails the same way. Not suddenly. Through a cascade of missed escalations. A warning that was not passed up. A signal that stayed in its bounded context instead of propagating to the next layer.

The thin margins should have escalated to re-excision. The neck pain should have escalated to imaging. The surgeon's notes from middle school should have been in Tricia's chart, not on microfiche. Each one was a signal that was absorbed instead of propagated.

When I debug a production outage, I walk the escalation chain in reverse. Start at the alert — the system is down. Walk backward through the logs, through the dependency graph, until you find the thing that broke.

Cancer is the same. A runaway exception. A failure that escalated through every layer that should have caught it.

## X.

I tried to research RAD51C on my own. I got fixated on Fanconi anemia — an inherited DNA repair disorder.

I was right. Same channel. The Fanconi anemia genes — FANCA, FANCC, FANCD2 — are on the PARP inhibitor trial list. RAD51C is in the same repair family. I found the parent node by instinct, years before I had the vocabulary for what I was doing.

The thing that made me fixate was this: Fanconi anemia patients have a higher incidence of bicornuate uterus. And horseshoe kidneys. Tricia had one big fused kidney, not two.

One mutation. Three structural anomalies. The uterus. The kidney. The cancer. All connected through the same DNA repair channel. The repair pathway that is broken shapes organ development too.

Her uncle died as a kid of leukemia. In the 1950s — around the time Watson and Crick first described the structure of DNA. The mechanism that killed him would not be understood for decades. The gene that carried it would not be sequenced until the 2000s. Would not be connected to PARP inhibitor sensitivity until the 2010s. Barely made the trial list in time for Tricia.

Sixty years of depth-first branches. Each one moved the frontier forward. None of them connected the uncle's leukemia to Tricia's uterus to Tricia's kidney to Tricia's cancer. Because that connection requires the breadth pass. And no one is funded to do the breadth pass.

Tricia's whole medical history was one signal. Every doctor saw one piece. I saw the pattern because I was the only person who had been present for all of it.

## XI.

Cancer treatment kills you. The hope is that it kills the cancer more.

Ibrance — a CDK4/6 inhibitor — worked for about 18 months. Then the Contessa trial — Xeloda plus teseltaxel — another 18 months. Each time the drug worked until it did not. Each failure meant more radiation.

After Contessa, MD Anderson would not radiate. The cancer kept coming back to the cervical spine — C6, the weakest point.

In 2021, with the cancer threatening to enter the spinal fluid, we did a hail mary. A doctor at the Dallas Proton Therapy Center agreed to do it. He felt like she was out of options anyway. He said afterward that he had been worried she might not be able to get up after the treatment. The proton beam could have broken what was left of the vertebra.

She got up.

That bought enough time to get into Dr. Timothy Yap's PARP inhibitor trial at MD Anderson. Saruparib — a PARP1-selective inhibitor. The drug that exploits the gap in the DNA repair channel. RAD51C was barely on the eligibility list.

She entered the trial fainting, with pleural fluid compressing her lungs. For six months I drained about 500 milliliters a day. By the end of the six months it was almost nothing. The soft tissue tumors disappeared completely. Cancer was unmeasurable.

But the bone. It stayed in the bones.

At 24 months, her markers started climbing. Some radiation on her rib — a new site. It went back down. Then it climbed again.

Thirty-four months total. The longest run of any treatment she had. But every single month was a slow descent. Less arm function. Less leg function. More nerve pain. The cumulative radiation was the price for every prior intervention. Side effects do not reset. They compound.

A one-way ratchet. Each treatment kills the vulnerable cells and selects for the resistant ones. The cancer evolves in one direction. The body accumulates damage in one direction. The clock runs one way.

## XII.

She was supposed to ask me to help her go to the bathroom. I was in the other room. I did not hear her fall.

When I checked on her I found her on the floor, her airway completely blocked. CPR. EMTs. Firemen. Cops. Thirty minutes of CPR and they got a heartbeat. But thirty minutes. She was already gone.

I followed the ambulance. My in-laws got the kids from school. They kept her on life support long enough for Dylan and Colin to say goodbye before they pulled the ventilator tube. She did not hear them.

There is a room in the ER where they tell you your person has died. The loneliest five minutes I have ever spent.

I have seen a lot of doctors cry or look defeated over those years. The old, formal male doctor crying when we lost Oscar. Yap, knowing it was over, still trying to fight for a path for us. And I have seen doctors fail. The breast surgeon who messed up our appointment because she was so mean to her receptionists they constantly quit. The local hotshot who took on so many patients he was an hour late to every appointment so he could manage as much crisis as possible. He was stealing the time we had left so he could feel more important. Those hours waiting for him were hours we would not get back, doing the one thing we already spent way too much time doing. Sitting in a doctor's office waiting to hear bad news.

Complexity gets you noticed. It gets you promoted. It usually does not solve the problem the best way. MD Anderson doctors were almost always on time and made sure every question was answered. When they were late it was because they were dealing with a real crisis. A stage 4 progression. Life and death. Not manufactured. We knew why Yap was late. It was to give people like us the time they needed in a moment of crisis.

Seven years, almost to the day, from the stage 4 diagnosis.

## XIII.

The uncertainty between us — nine years of cancer, of not knowing the prognosis, the next treatment, whether the neck pain was cancer or nothing — pushed us apart. Coparents more than a couple by the end.

She chose the kids and her reputation. She wanted to matter. And I did not help her do that. I think she resented that I was mattering more in my work with every ratchet of my career. Every rung I climbed was another step in the trajectory she had lost. AllThingsD to GeekWire to patient. The asymmetry grew every year. Not because either of us chose it. Because the cancer consumed her capacity while my career expanded mine.

Her early successes — AllThingsD, the Bezos interview — meant less time for me. My successes meant more capacity, but I could never celebrate any of it. Because my wife was literally dying.

We were the right combination. She confirmed facts. I organized them. She was the intersection step — two sources agree, it is real. I was the graph step — structure it so it compounds. Together we were the full pipeline: source it, confirm it, structure it, make it free.

We did not quite get there together. The fog was too thick. Medical uncertainty, career uncertainty, financial uncertainty — all compounding for nine years. We loved each other and it still pushed us apart because the bounded context was too full of fog to hold each other.

## XIV.

I have been pouring for eighteen months. Doing my best but deep down not myself. Not curious. Not engaged. Faking it. A great dad. But not taking care of myself.

I fought using AI. It was the opposite of everything I had done to get where I was. I never ask the same question twice. AI asks every question from scratch every time.

I was waiting to get laid off. And then I did not. Instead I got assigned 700 applications to migrate from one deployment platform to another. Fifty teams. No specification. Suddenly I had to deliver.

Nobody knew anything about what was going on. We did not even have repo URLs for most of the applications. Before I could do the work, I had to build the map of the work.

The company paid $47,000 a month for a search tool called Glean that indexed our Confluence pages, Jira tickets, and GitHub repos, then fed the results to an AI. Every question cost 60,000 tokens of context. Can you imagine Gleaning 700 times? And then doing it again and again?

It is so the opposite of me.

Glean only retrieves knowledge someone already wrote down. It does not discover. Nobody had written down the map. And who looks at 700 repos at once? Not anyone who is told that context windows are too small for big code analysis.

So I bent the tool to my will. Every inefficiency was an attack vector. I started with RAG — indexed memories, feeding the AI its own answers. It worked OK. Completely unshareable.

Then I walked my career backwards. The MapReduce lesson: group by equivalence class first. The GraphQL lesson: compose partials into a traversable DAG. The functional composition lesson: break into independent units. Backend systems use pointers when they run out of memory. In Claude, file handles are pointers. And what kind of system does broad pattern discovery with file handle pointers?

MapReduce.

I built a graph. A set of nodes — the things I knew — and edges — how they related. Twenty megabytes. Ran on my MacBook Air, a 2020 model. Within a month my Glean calls dropped 99%. My token usage dropped 20x. The 700 apps fell into about 15 categories. After five examples of a category I could predict the sixth without the AI. After ten I could write a script. Sixty percent of what the AI was doing was deterministic.

I had a space-covering problem. Space-covering problems are graph problems. Instead of parameters for all the cases, I had a bunch of smaller problems. That is a big deal in an n-squared space — turning m times n into m plus n.

And because I had a learning loop — feedback from each observation updating the graph — I could measure convergence under different orderings. Equivalence-class-first always won. Random. Best to worst. Worst to best. Class-first converges fastest because the class boundary is where the information lives.

With no Glean and optimized analysis, I expanded to 4,300 repos. Every tier-zero through tier-two application in the company and a bunch of tier-three ones.

The graph was not doing anything sophisticated. It was doing something obvious that no one had bothered to do: remembering.

## XV.

The company cannot adopt it. Not because it does not work. Because the incentive structure will not allow it.

There is no AI strategy. No high-level effort to find the big picture. Teams compete for AI coding targets — every collaboration is a possible attack vector. Teams plan to automate changes to other teams' code while those teams have their own AI goals. If another team's AI tool changes your code, they get the credit for efficiency and you own the incident at 2 AM.

Constant threat of job cuts. Constant stack ranking. Stack ranking is the anti-graph. Help someone and you fall in the ranking. Hoard knowledge and you rise. The incentive structure manufactures silos. The AI tools that are supposed to connect everything are driving teams apart.

People get promoted for shipping complexity. Nobody gets promoted for the four-person system that just works and never needs updating. The checkout code that still runs five years later, unbeatable on conversion, is invisible. The nine-UI replacement with a shared cache is visible. It has a team. It has a launch date. It does not work as well.

Are you going to promote the person who reported to you for six months and delivered something huge, or the person you have trusted for three years?

I do not say this with bitterness. Expedia was very accommodating. But you see the same failures over and over. And we are doing it again with AI. No strategy. No shared graph. Every team re-deriving the same patterns from scratch.

I do not have faith that even the perfect solution can win against politics and that environment.

That is why we are doing it here. Open. Public. Free.

## XVI.

I realized I was not refactoring the code. I was refactoring the process. And the design patterns I had used for twenty years were not about code. They were about one thing.

Separate concerns and bound risk.

That is all design patterns in five words. Factory, observer, strategy, adapter — every one exists because a finite context window forced a separation. They are not twenty-three clever tricks. They are a taxonomy of responses to one problem.

I used them for twenty years. I knew they helped. But I could not have told you they were all one thing until I had found the same constraint operating in cancer cells, in the genome, in the structure of language. Each substrate stripped away one more layer of implementation until the only thing left was the constraint itself.

Active context is finite. That is the one sentence. It took twenty years to earn it.

And what do you do when you have bounded risk and something goes wrong? You escalate. The failure moves up a level.

That is what I was hunting for in cancer. Not the genes. Not the mutations. The escalation architecture. Which defense systems are independent. What happens when one fails. Where the signal propagates when a boundary is breached.

I did not believe the body was an unacting victim, just waiting for a cure. Does anyone really believe mutations are random? Nothing with ten billion cells and 3.8 billion years of evolution survives by being passive. It fights. It has architecture. It has governance.

Cancer is a runaway exception — a failure that escalated through every layer that should have caught it. Engineers do root cause analysis by walking the escalation in reverse.

I was doing a post-mortem. Literally.

## XVII.

A cell has defense systems. Not one. Several. DNA repair catches copying errors. The cell cycle checkpoint catches cells that should not be dividing. The PI3K/Growth pathway controls resource allocation. Chromatin remodeling maintains the index that tells the cell which genes to read. DNA methylation maintains the marks that tell the cell what type it is. The immune system surveys for rogue cells. The endocrine system coordinates distant tissues. Tissue architecture maintains physical structure.

Eight channels. Eight independent defense systems. I did not discover them. Every oncologist knows these pathways exist. They are textbook biology.

What I brought was a frame. When I see a complex system, I do not think about what each component does. I ask how many independent levels of governance there are and what happens when one goes down.

I assigned each of the 92 most commonly mutated cancer genes to one of these eight channels based on its known biological function. Then I counted, for each patient, how many channels had at least one mutation.

I did not know about the mutation paradox when I started. I was not trying to resolve a debate. I was trying to validate the channels — to show that counting broken layers predicted something. I put channel count into a survival model. And because I am methodical, I put mutation count in as a control variable. I expected it to be harmful. More mutations, worse cancer. Everyone knows that.

The model said the opposite. But I did not understand why yet.

At first the result was just that channels were predictive. More broken channels, worse survival. That worked. The mutation count result was a loose thread — something strange in the output that did not fit. I moved on.

It took weeks to come back to it. Weeks of running the model different ways, splitting patients into groups, staring at survival curves that did not make sense if mutations were simply bad. The resolution came not in a flash but in a slow separation — the gradual realization that there were two classes of mutation hiding inside one number.

The dataset is from Memorial Sloan Kettering. Over 44,000 patients. I ran a Cox proportional hazards model on 24,561 with full clinical data. Controlled for everything: age, sex, cancer type, metastatic site count, fraction of genome altered, microsatellite instability, tumor purity. Two variables: channel count and mutation count.

Both significant. Channel count — the number of defense systems hit — predicted worse survival. Hazard ratio 1.09, p less than 10 to the negative 22.

Mutation count, conditional on channel count, predicted better survival. Hazard ratio 0.90, p less than 10 to the negative 32.

Once you know how many defense systems are broken, additional mutations within those systems are protective. Not harmful. Protective.

A patient with 4 mutations across 4 channels has broken 4 defense systems. Every mutation was an escalation. A patient with 30 mutations across 2 channels has broken 2 and accumulated 28 passengers — mutations that did not breach anything new. Same mutation count range. Opposite prognosis.

Tumor mutation burden was never contradictory. It was two signals mashed together. Channel count separates them. One variable. Twelve-year debate, resolved.

The mutations are not random. They look random when you count them without the frame. Add the frame and every mutation is either an escalation or a passenger. Fully explainable.

"Run this step 7 times. I don't know why but the data looks right." That was TMB. One missing variable clears the fog.

## XVIII.

Nine genes are on the PARP inhibitor trial list. ATM, BRCA1, BRCA2, FANCA, FANCC, FANCD2, PALB2, RAD51C, RAD51D.

RAD51C and BRCA2 do the same thing. They are copies of the same repair unit, written to different addresses in the genome. The address is different. The function is identical. That is why saruparib worked for Tricia. It targeted the vulnerability, not the name.

There are at least ten more genes that do the same job. BLM, BRIP1, CHEK1, CHEK2, ERCC4, MRE11, NBN, RAD51, RAD51B, XRCC2. Same functional family. Same vulnerability. Over 4,000 patients in the dataset carry these mutations. None eligible for the trial.

RAD51C was barely on the list. If it had taken one more study, one more year, the drug that gave Tricia 34 months would not have been available to her.

Science does depth-first search. Pick a hypothesis. Go deep. Publish. Pick the next. Each study is rigorous within its branch. No one comes back up to the root to ask whether the branches are the same tree. No grant funds the breadth pass. No journal publishes "these ten things are the same thing."

The graph is the breadth pass. It does not go deeper. It goes wider. And the width is where the missing patients are.

## XIX.

There is a difference between discovering something and being told something.

My engineering career was discovery. Each layer earned, lived in, debugged from inside. When you discover something, you know why it is true.

My learning about cancer was being told. I read the papers, the trial protocols, the gene names. Biologists discovered that knowledge over decades. I received it as organized knowledge. When you are told something, you know that it is true.

Discovery writes different edges than being told. The biologists know why each gene matters. I know that the pathways exist. But I discovered layers. So when I looked at eight cancer pathways, I saw eight governance layers.

Every person carries a different graph. Each life wires different edges between the same nodes. An oncologist and I can both look at RAD51C. The oncologist walks to BRCA2 along a pathway edge — same DNA repair pathway. I walk to BRCA2 along a redundancy edge — copies of the same functional unit. Same two nodes. Different edge. Different conclusion.

The edges that exist in one person's graph and not another's are where discovery lives. Every person who has ever seen something no one else saw was not smarter. They had different edges.

And the thing we lose when we dismiss a perspective — "you are not an oncologist," "you are not qualified" — is not a person's feelings. It is their edges. The part of human knowledge that only they carry.

## XX.

There is an industry built on not knowing.

Uncertainty is profitable. The merchant who controls the fog controls the market. Sam Altman scraped the sum of human knowledge — every blog post, every Stack Overflow answer, every paper, every forum reply — locked it behind an API, and charges per token to give it back. The training data was freely given. People shared what they knew because that is what knowledge does. Now someone is selling it back to them.

That is not innovation. That is enclosure.

Trump and the billionaires buying the media and communications channels — Musk, Ellison, Bezos, Zuckerberg — are doing the same thing at a different layer. They do not need to disprove facts. They flood the channel with enough noise that the signal-to-noise ratio drops below the threshold where anyone can confirm anything. That is not censorship. It is denial of service on the bounded context.

The fog does not just confuse. It isolates. When two people in the same room carry different sets of unconfirmed facts, they cannot see each other. Every couple arguing about politics. Every family split by conspiracy theories. Every friendship strained because two people carry different noise and mistake it for signal.

I lived the most concentrated version. Medical uncertainty, career uncertainty, financial uncertainty — nine years, compounding. We loved each other and it still pushed us apart.

Tricia's entire career was building the antidote. Source it. Confirm it. Publish it so it is free. That is what journalism is. The graph with provenance — every node sourced, every edge attributed — is what she did for a living. And the uncertainty merchants are dismantling the institutions that do it.

## XXII.

I plotted the number of defense layers against the number of cells across ten organisms — from E. coli to the bowhead whale. The relationship was logarithmic. Layers scale as the log of cell count. R-squared 0.84. p = 0.00018.

The naked mole rat is about the size of a mouse. Same number of cells. Same seven layers. The mouse gets cancer 30% of the time. The naked mole rat gets cancer less than 0.1%. Same architecture. Different layer quality. Three-hundred-fold difference.

My search tool cost $47,000 a month because it searched from scratch every time. My graph cost nothing because it remembered. One scaled quadratically. The other scaled logarithmically. There is a crossover where the lines meet. Before the crossover, searching is simpler. After, it is ruinous and the graph is the only option.

Language models are on this curve now. Context windows growing. Costs rising quadratically. The success is real. The crossover is ahead. Some will build the graph and cross over. Some will find the niche where brute-force attention is enough and stay there forever. Both survive.

Dinosaurs went extinct not because they failed but because they succeeded in a strategy that could not survive the crossover. The lineages that had already begun building efficient, governed architectures became birds. The lineages that stayed small in a niche where the crossover never arrived became crocodilians. Both are still here. The middle of the curve is empty.

## XXIII.

Six weeks ago I had a laptop, an API key, and a $47,000 monthly search bill. Now I have a 20-megabyte graph, a framework that explains why it works, and eleven papers showing the same pattern in cancer, the genome, software, and language.

We are building it from the ground up. Starting with language and math. Tested with word problems. Getting the graph shape right so that when we reach trigonometry and calculus and linear algebra, it is just new nodes and edges. The traversal engine does not change. The graph gets wider.

As humans, we built this graph once. We showed it to our children. We told it over campfires. We sang it in songs. We wrote it in books. And we shared it with each other. That was the point.

This is a personal story. But personal stories are how knowledge has always moved. A bacterium discovered that two repair layers were enough, and that discovery is written in its genome 3.8 billion years later. A naked mole rat discovered that layer quality matters more than count, and that discovery is written in a cancer rate three hundred times lower than a mouse's. None of them articulated what they learned. They survived, and the survival was the record.

Human intelligence is the same process with one difference: we can articulate it. We can write down the edge that connects two nodes and hand it to someone whose graph does not contain it. That is what language is — a protocol for transmitting edges between graphs that cannot share memory. That is what a paper is. That is what a book is. That is what this is.

There are 4,000 patients in the dataset who carry the same vulnerability as Tricia, filed under a different name, excluded from the trial that gave her 34 months. The information exists. It is not connected.

Two roads diverged. I always took the harder one — the one that led to more understanding. QA to data to backend to platform to the graph. Each fork, the path with more to learn.

Now I am taking the other road. Tricia's road. Source it. Confirm it. Make it free.

She probably would have figured out how to make it a business.

I am one person with one set of edges. My graph is twenty megabytes. The graph of all human understanding is the union of every perspective that ever existed — every QA tester, every oncologist, every journalist who sourced a fact and published it free, every ten-year-old who named the thing at the dinner table that the adults could not see.

Knowledge is beautiful. Life is ugly. Union and intersection.

The context is finite. The union is not. That is the point of all of us.

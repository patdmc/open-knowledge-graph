\title{Coupling-Channel Count Predicts Cancer Survival\\Better Than Mutation Count}
\author{Patrick D.\ McCarthy}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We show that the number of distinct organizational systems
(``coupling channels'') disrupted by a tumor's mutations predicts
overall survival significantly better than the total number of
mutations. Across three independent clinical genomic datasets
totaling 73{,}593 patients, coupling-channel count is a significant
predictor of survival in multivariate Cox models ($p < 0.005$) while
mutation count either loses significance or becomes \emph{protective}
(HR\,$=$\,0.82--0.83). Patients whose mutations hit different
channels have worse outcomes than patients with the same number of
mutations confined to one channel ($p < 10^{-5}$ in all three
datasets). The signal survives cancer-type adjustment (1.1\%
attenuation), a 275-covariate kitchen-sink Cox model
($p = 10^{-23}$), restriction to truncating and hotspot missense
variants (HR strengthens from 1.15 to 1.19), and comparison against
data-driven gene groupings (which fail: ARI\,$=$\,0.018, HR in the
wrong direction).

Coupling channels are severed in a consistent order:
cell-intrinsic growth control first, organism-level immune
surveillance last. Among patients with two or more channels severed,
98.1\% have at least one cell-intrinsic gateway channel. The 1.9\%
who enter through organism-level channels are enriched for
hormone-dependent cancers (prostate, breast) and have lower
mortality.

A coupling channel is not a biochemical pathway. It is the
organizational function that a pathway serves. We identify six
channels and map mutations to them using public pathway databases.
The variable that predicts prognosis is the number of independent
organizational functions the tumor has escaped, not the number of
molecular events. This empirical result implies structural claims
about multicellular organization, bounded context, escalation
hierarchies, knowledge-action inseparability, that are stated here
and proved formally in companion papers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Counting mutations does not predict cancer survival. Counting
the number of distinct organizational systems those mutations
disrupt does.

We tested this across three independent clinical genomic datasets
totaling 73{,}593 patients (Section~\ref{sec:empirical}). For each
patient, we mapped somatic mutations to six organizational
functions, what we call \textbf{coupling channels}, and counted
how many channels were disrupted. In multivariate Cox regression,
channel count is a significant predictor of overall survival in
every dataset ($p < 0.005$). Mutation count alone is either
non-significant (MetTropism: $p = 0.69$) or weakly significant.
In the multivariate model including both predictors, channel count
remains highly significant in all three datasets. Mutation
count either drops to non-significance (MSK-IMPACT 2017:
$p = 0.67$) or becomes \emph{protective} (MetTropism:
HR\,$=$\,0.82; MSK-IMPACT 50K: HR\,$=$\,0.83; both $p < 0.005$).
The protective effect of additional mutations, conditional on
channel count, is the key finding: more mutations in the same
channel means deeper commitment to one escape route, not
diversification across channels.

\textbf{What is a coupling channel?} A coupling channel is not a
biochemical pathway. It is the organizational function that a
pathway serves. DNA damage repair is a pathway. The organizational
function it serves, maintaining genome integrity so that tissue can
trust the cell's replication fidelity, is a coupling channel.
The PI3K--AKT--mTOR cascade is a pathway. The organizational
function it serves, coupling cell growth to tissue-level growth
factor signals, is a coupling channel. Multiple pathways can serve
the same channel. One pathway can participate in multiple channels.
The variable that predicts prognosis is not the number of molecular
events but the number of independent organizational functions the
tumor has escaped.

Cancer is \textbf{escalation chain severance}. We model
multicellular organization as a hierarchy maintained by coupling
channels that connect cells to tissue, organ, and organism levels.
Severing these channels forces cell-level optimization, producing
the convergent cancer phenotype.

The empirical results imply structural claims about how
multicellular organization works: that context is
bounded, that control converges to escalation hierarchies, that
knowledge and action are inseparable. These claims are stated in
Section~\ref{sec:framework} and proved formally in
\cite{McCarthy2026a, McCarthy2026b, McCarthy2026c, McCarthy2026d}.
This paper can be read independently of the formal proofs.

\textbf{Paper structure.} Section~\ref{sec:empirical} presents the
empirical result and seven robustness tests.
Section~\ref{sec:framework} provides a structural interpretation.
Section~\ref{sec:prior} engages prior work.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Empirical Result: Channel Count Predicts Survival}
\label{sec:empirical}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

We tested the central claim, that channel count predicts survival
better than mutation count, using three publicly available datasets
from cBioPortal \cite{Cerami2012}: the MSK-IMPACT Clinical
Sequencing Cohort ($n = 7{,}091$ patients, 2{,}126 deaths;
pan-cancer) \cite{Zehir2017}, the MSK MetTropism Cohort
($n = 24{,}646$ patients, 9{,}893 deaths; metastatic)
\cite{Nguyen2022}, and the MSK-IMPACT 50K Cohort
($n = 41{,}856$ patients, 16{,}731 deaths; pan-cancer)
\cite{Bandlamudi2024}. Together: 73{,}593 patients, 28{,}750 deaths.

\subsection{Method}

For each patient, we mapped somatic mutations to six coupling
channels based on the organizational function of the mutated gene:
(1)~DNA damage response (DDR: \emph{BRCA1}, \emph{BRCA2},
\emph{ATM}, \emph{CHEK2}, mismatch repair genes),
(2)~cell cycle checkpoint (\emph{TP53}, \emph{RB1}, \emph{CDKN2A},
\emph{CDK4/6}),
(3)~growth signaling (PI3K--AKT--mTOR, RAS--MAPK:
\emph{PIK3CA}, \emph{PTEN}, \emph{KRAS}, \emph{BRAF}, \emph{EGFR},
\emph{ERBB2}),
(4)~endocrine/hormone (\emph{ESR1}, \emph{GATA3}, \emph{FOXA1},
\emph{AR}),
(5)~immune surveillance (\emph{B2M}, \emph{HLA-A/B/C},
\emph{JAK1/2}),
(6)~tissue architecture (\emph{CDH1}, \emph{APC}, \emph{CTNNB1},
\emph{SMAD4}, \emph{NOTCH1}).
Non-silent mutations (missense, nonsense, frameshift, splice site)
in these genes. For each patient, we recorded the number of
\emph{distinct channels} with at least one mutation.
The channel mapping and analysis code are provided in the
supplementary repository.

\subsection{Results}

\textbf{Channel count predicts survival; mutation count does not.}
Table~\ref{tab:empirical} summarizes the Cox proportional hazards
results across all three datasets. In every dataset, channel count
is a significant predictor of overall survival ($p < 0.005$).
Mutation count alone is either non-significant (MetTropism:
$p = 0.69$) or weakly significant. In the multivariate model
including both predictors, channel count remains highly significant
in all three datasets. Mutation count either drops to
non-significance (MSK-IMPACT 2017: $p = 0.67$) or becomes
\emph{protective} (MetTropism: HR $= 0.82$; 50K: HR $= 0.83$;
both $p < 0.005$). The protective effect of additional mutations,
conditional on channel count, is the most important result in
Table~\ref{tab:empirical}. If channel count simply proxied for
tumor burden or advanced stage, mutation count should also predict
worse outcomes. It does the opposite. This rules out the class
of confounders where ``more mutations = more advanced disease.''
The signal is specifically about organizational-function disruption,
not about mutational burden.

\textbf{Same-channel depth: more mutations in fewer channels is
protective.} We stratified patients by fixed channel count and
compared survival between low and high mutation-count quartiles.
In patients with 2--3 channels severed, those with more mutations
survive significantly longer. In the 50K cohort: at 2~channels,
mortality drops from 46.2\% to 41.2\% ($p = 3.1 \times 10^{-8}$);
at 3~channels, from 46.9\% to 38.6\%
($p = 7.2 \times 10^{-14}$). MetTropism replicates: at 3~channels,
49.6\% vs.\ 36.5\% ($p = 2.6 \times 10^{-10}$). Once a tumor has
severed $k$~channels, additional mutations that stay within those
channels represent deeper commitment to an already-chosen
trajectory: a more constrained, predictable, and treatable tumor.

\textbf{Mortality gradient by channel count.} All three datasets
show a monotonic mortality gradient from 0 to 3--4 channels
severed (Figure~\ref{fig:km}). In the 50K cohort: 30.4\% (0~channels), 38.5\% (1),
44.2\% (2), 44.3\% (3), 40.3\% (4). Patients with 5--6 channels
show lower mortality (29.0\%, 20.7\%), consistent with the
hypermutation phenotype (MSI-high, mismatch repair deficient) where
most mutations are passengers that do not functionally sever channels.

is one hypothesis. To test robustness, we repeated the multivariate
Cox regression at every granularity from 2 to 10 channels.
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity} shows the results for the MSK-IMPACT
50K cohort (the two smaller datasets replicate the same pattern).

The signal is significant at every granularity from 2 to 8 and peaks
at 3~channels ($p = 3 \times 10^{-124}$). At 2~channels, the
grouping is too coarse: both channel count and mutation count are
significant because the two mega-channels still contain meaningful
internal variation. From 3 to 8, channel count absorbs the
predictive power of mutation count, which becomes protective or
non-significant. At 10~channels, over-splitting within
organizational functions makes ``channel count'' collapse back into
a proxy for mutation count.

The peak at 3~channels (genome integrity, growth signaling,
microenvironment) suggests that the coarsest biologically meaningful
grouping captures most of the signal. The specific number of
channels is less important than the principle: grouping mutations by
organizational function outpredicts counting them, and the optimal
granularity is determined by the actual independence structure of
the coupling hierarchy.

\textbf{Cancer-type adjustment.} A natural objection is that channel
count proxies for cancer type: lung cancers have more mutations than
thyroid cancers, and different cancer types have different prognoses.
We tested this by adding cancer type as a covariate (one-hot encoded,
38~types with $n \geq 50$) to the multivariate Cox model. In the
50K cohort, the channel-count hazard ratio barely changes: HR~$= 1.17$
($p = 8 \times 10^{-73}$) vs.\ HR~$= 1.17$ ($p = 1 \times 10^{-88}$)
unadjusted, an attenuation of 1.1\%. MetTropism shows 16\% attenuation
but remains significant ($p = 2 \times 10^{-47}$). MSK-IMPACT 2017
shows no attenuation. Channel count is not a proxy for cancer type.

Stratified analysis within individual cancer types confirms this.
Channel count is significant within non-small cell lung cancer
(HR~$= 1.16$, $p = 3 \times 10^{-13}$), breast cancer
(HR~$= 1.29$, $p = 1 \times 10^{-21}$), prostate cancer
(HR~$= 1.47$, $p = 9 \times 10^{-21}$), and pancreatic cancer
(HR~$= 1.32$, $p = 2 \times 10^{-19}$). Prostate cancer shows
the strongest within-type effect, consistent with its narrow
channel architecture: most prostate tumors are concentrated on a
single coupling axis, so each additional channel severed represents
a larger relative loss of organizational control.

\textbf{Full covariate adjustment.} To test whether channel count
captures anything beyond known prognostic variables, we ran a
kitchen-sink Cox model controlling for every available clinical
attribute simultaneously. Covariates include tumor mutational burden (TMB), MSI score
and type, cancer type and detailed subtype, age at diagnosis, sex,
tumor purity, fraction genome altered, primary site, sample type,
and gene panel version. In the 50K cohort, this produces 275
covariates. Channel count remains significant: HR~$= 1.09$,
$p = 1 \times 10^{-23}$. MetTropism (187~covariates):
HR~$= 1.11$, $p = 1 \times 10^{-18}$. MSK-IMPACT 2017
(109~covariates): HR~$= 1.14$, $p = 2 \times 10^{-8}$.
After controlling for everything available in three clinical
genomic databases, channel count still predicts survival.

\textbf{Severance hierarchy: cell-intrinsic channels first.}
The six coupling channels are not severed at random
(Figure~\ref{fig:hierarchy}). Among patients
with exactly one channel severed ($n = 12{,}558$), 80\% have
PI3K/Growth Signaling or Cell Cycle: the cell-intrinsic channels
that govern proliferation. Endocrine and Immune account for fewer
than 5\%. Among patients with two or more channels severed
($n = 24{,}535$), 98.1\% have at least one cell-intrinsic channel.

The progression ladder is:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1~channel}: PI3K/Growth (44.5\%) or Cell Cycle
  (35.7\%) dominate. Immune: 0.7\%.
\item \textbf{2~channels}: PI3K/Growth rises to 73.9\%, Cell Cycle
  to 64.6\%. Tissue Architecture enters at 33.6\%.
\item \textbf{3~channels}: PI3K/Growth at 90.8\%, Cell Cycle 80.9\%,
  Tissue Architecture 70.8\%. Endocrine (13.5\%) and Immune (7.2\%)
  remain rare.
\item \textbf{4~channels}: PI3K/Growth at 96.7\%. Endocrine and
  Immune are the last additions (30\%, 25\%).
\end{itemize}

The ordering is PI3K/Growth $\to$ Cell Cycle $\to$ Tissue
Architecture $\to$ DDR $\to$ Endocrine $\to$ Immune. Growth escape
is the gateway; immune evasion is the last system to fall. This is
not a statistical artifact of channel frequency. Cell Cycle is more
common than Tissue Architecture overall, yet at one channel they
have similar representation (35.7\% vs.\ 8.5\%), because the
single-channel patients reflect first hits, not base rates.

This ordering is the escalation chain read in reverse. The channels
severed first (PI3K/Growth, Cell Cycle) are the cell-tissue
boundary: the coupling mechanisms that say ``do not divide without
tissue permission.'' The channels severed last (Endocrine, Immune)
are organism-level controls that operate at levels~2--3 of the
hierarchy. A cell cannot meaningfully escape immune surveillance or
endocrine regulation before it has escaped the basic growth
constraint. The severance hierarchy is the empirical signature of
escalation ordering (Section~\ref{sec:framework}): the first
coupling to break is the lowest level of the chain, and progression
moves upward through the hierarchy as additional channels fall.

The 1.9\% of multi-channel patients who lack a cell-intrinsic
gateway channel are not noise. They are enriched 6.5$\times$ for
prostate cancer, 2.5$\times$ for breast cancer, and 7.6$\times$ for
mesothelioma: cancers that enter through organism-level channels
(endocrine, tissue architecture) rather than the standard
growth-escape gateway. These tumors severed higher-level coupling
first while leaving cell-level growth control partially intact,
consistent with multiple entry points into the escalation chain.
Their lower mortality (34.8\% vs.\ 42.6\% for all multi-channel
patients) is predicted: they have not yet escaped the most
fundamental coupling layer.

\textbf{The grouping must be biological, not statistical.} A second
objection is that any gene grouping would produce a survival signal:
pick genes, cluster them, count clusters, and the count will predict
something. We tested this directly. Using hierarchical clustering on
Jaccard co-mutation distances across the 92 channel genes with
sufficient mutation frequency ($n \geq 100$), we derived data-driven
gene groupings at every granularity from $k = 2$ to $k = 10$ and
tested each against survival.

The data-driven groupings fail. At $k = 6$, the data-driven cluster
count produces HR~$= 0.80$ ($p = 9 \times 10^{-6}$): more clusters
hit predicts \emph{better} survival, the wrong direction. The
hand-picked biological channels at $k = 6$ produce HR~$= 1.10$
($p = 3 \times 10^{-9}$). The adjusted Rand index between
data-driven and hand-picked groupings is 0.018, effectively zero
overlap.

Co-mutation patterns group genes by which tumors they appear in
(driven by panel coverage, cancer type, and mutational processes),
not by organizational function. The biological channel mapping
captures something that statistical co-occurrence does not: which
organizational system a gene serves. Not just any grouping works.
The grouping must reflect the coupling architecture.

\textbf{Pathogenic variant restriction.} The baseline analysis
counts any non-silent mutation as a severance event, including
variants of unknown significance (VUS). To test whether VUS inflate
the signal, we repeated the analysis under two stricter filters:
truncating mutations only (nonsense, frameshift, splice site) and
truncating plus hotspot missense (recurrent positions with $\geq 5$
patients, capturing KRAS~G12, BRAF~V600, PIK3CA~H1047,
TP53~hotspots, etc.). The signal strengthens: channel-count
HR rises from 1.15 (all non-silent) to 1.16 (truncating only) to
1.19 (truncating + hotspot; $p = 1 \times 10^{-55}$). Mutation
count becomes more protective under stricter filtering (HR~$= 0.75$
vs.\ 0.83 baseline). Removing VUS concentrates the signal; it does
not create it. The severance hierarchy is preserved under
truncating-only filtering: PI3K/Growth and Cell Cycle dominate at
one channel, Endocrine and Immune remain late additions.

\textbf{Panel bias.} MSK-IMPACT sequences 400--500 genes, a
fraction of the genome. Mutations in channel genes not on the panel
are undetected, systematically undercounting channel hits. This
compresses the channel-count gradient and works against the
hypothesis, not for it. The true association between channel count
and survival is likely stronger than what the panel captures.

\textbf{Limitations.} The analysis does not distinguish hub from
leaf mutations within a channel, nor does it adjust for stage or
treatment. The optimal channel count is an empirical question that
finer-grained pathway databases could resolve. Nevertheless, the
core result is robust across three independent datasets, eight
granularities, within individual cancer types, after controlling for
275 clinical covariates, under three mutation-filtering stringencies,
and against data-driven alternatives: counting organizational systems
disrupted predicts survival better than counting disruptions.

\section{Structural Interpretation}
\label{sec:framework}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Why does organizational-function disruption predict survival better
than mutational burden? The empirical results of
Section~\ref{sec:empirical} are consistent with a structural model:
cancer as escalation chain severance under bounded context. The
formal derivations appear in
\cite{McCarthy2026a, McCarthy2026b, McCarthy2026c, McCarthy2026d};
here we state the interpretation in biological terms.

\textbf{Bounded context and escalation.}
Any information-processing entity has a hard limit on the number of
regulatory states it can simultaneously maintain ($C_n$). A cell can
monitor a finite number of signaling pathways concurrently; an
organism's working memory holds $4 \pm 1$ items \cite{Cowan2001}.
Multicellular organization overcomes this limit through hierarchical
coupling: a cell ($k = 0$) escalates problems exceeding its capacity
to tissue ($k = 1$), organ ($k = 2$), and organism ($k = 3$) levels
\cite{McCarthy2026b}. Through intact coupling, the cell's effective
context exceeds its intrinsic limit.

\textbf{Coupling channels as the coupling mechanism.}
The coupling between levels is not monolithic. It decomposes into
independent channels: gap junctions and cadherins at the cell-tissue
boundary \cite{Aasen2016, Hanahan2011}, paracrine signaling and ECM
architecture at the tissue-organ boundary, endocrine signaling and
immune surveillance at the organ-organism boundary. These are the
six coupling channels of Section~\ref{sec:empirical}. Each channel
carries a different class of organizational information; losing one
does not imply losing the others.

\textbf{Chain severance produces convergent malignancy.}
When a coupling channel is lost, the cell's effective context
contracts. Regulatory states that depended on tissue-level or
organism-level information lose their grounding and are pruned
\cite{McCarthy2026a}. The action space contracts with the knowledge
base \cite{McCarthy2026c}: actions requiring tissue-level
coordination (contact inhibition, apoptosis on organismal command,
differentiated quiescence) become inaccessible. What remains are
actions executable with cell-level knowledge alone: proliferation,
metabolic autonomy, motility. This accounts for why Hanahan and
Weinberg's hallmarks \cite{Hanahan2011} are observed across cancer
types: they are the convergent action space under cell-level bounded
context.

The Warburg effect illustrates this directly. Cancer cells use
aerobic glycolysis even when oxygen is present. Not because oxygen
is unavailable, but because after severance the cell lacks
tissue-level information about oxygen supply reliability and defaults
to the metabolic strategy that requires no such information
\cite{Warburg1956}.

\textbf{Why channel count predicts survival.}
Each additional channel severed removes an independent class of
organizational coupling. The cell's effective context shrinks
further, its action space narrows further, and its optimization
regime converges further toward the cell-level attractor. More
channels severed means more complete decoupling from the organism.
Mutation count, by contrast, measures depth within channels already
severed, which is why it becomes protective conditional on channel
count: deeper commitment to fewer escape routes produces a more
constrained, predictable tumor.

\textbf{Why channels are severed in order.}
The severance hierarchy (PI3K/Growth first, Immune last) follows
from the escalation architecture. Cell-tissue coupling
(growth signaling, cell cycle control) is the lowest level of the
hierarchy and the first to sever. Organism-level coupling (endocrine,
immune) operates at higher levels and cannot be meaningfully
exploited until cell-level escape is already achieved. The hierarchy
is the escalation chain read in reverse.

Chain severance can occur through multiple mechanisms: genetic
(receptor damage), microenvironmental (signal loss), epigenetic
(promoter silencing), or spatial (basement membrane breach). All
produce the same outcome
(Table~\ref{tab:severance-mechanisms}).
This explains why cancers arising from different mechanisms
(hereditary, environmental, sporadic) converge on the same
phenotypic endpoints.

\section{Engagement with Prior Work}
\label{sec:prior}

Table~\ref{tab:prior-work} summarizes the relationship between
existing cancer frameworks and chain severance.

The closest existing framework is TOFT \cite{Sonnenschein2000}.
It correctly identifies the organizational level (tissue, not cell)
and the default proliferative state. Our framework provides the
formalism TOFT has lacked: the escalation chain is the formal
structure of tissue organization, $C_n^{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the
measure of organizational integration, and chain severance is
the formal description of organizational breakdown. TOFT's
``default proliferative state'' is our ``necessary action space
under cell-level $C_n$.''

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Counting organizational systems disrupted predicts cancer survival
better than counting mutations. This result is robust across three
independent datasets (73{,}593 patients), eight channel
granularities, within individual cancer types, after adjusting for
275 clinical covariates, under three mutation-filtering stringencies,
and against data-driven alternatives. Coupling channels are severed
in escalation order: cell-intrinsic first, organism-level last.

The structural interpretation is chain severance: a cell that loses
its coupling channels loses the effective context those channels
provided and converges on cell-level optimization. The formal
derivations appear in companion papers
\cite{McCarthy2026a, McCarthy2026b, McCarthy2026c, McCarthy2026d}.

The immediate question the empirical result raises is whether
finer-grained distinctions within channels, specifically the
position of a mutation in the signaling graph (hub vs.\ leaf), carry
additional prognostic information beyond channel count alone. If so,
the coupling-channel framework generates predictions about cancer
subtype, metastatic tropism, and combination therapy design that are
testable against these same datasets.

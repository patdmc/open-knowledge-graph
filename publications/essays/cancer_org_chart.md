# Cancer Is an Org Chart Problem

## What happens when one employee goes rogue, and nobody catches it in time

---

Imagine a company. Ten thousand employees. Most of them do their jobs. They follow procedures, file accurate reports, respond to their managers. The company works because each person trusts the system above them to set priorities and trusts the people below them to execute.

Now imagine one employee starts cutting corners. Not dramatically. Not embezzlement. Just quiet quitting. Fudging a number on a report here. Skipping a quality check there. Marking a task complete when it isn't.

Nobody notices. The manager above them sees the same green checkmarks in the dashboard. The reports look fine.

This is how cancer starts.

## The fudged report

A cell in your body is an employee in an organization. It has a job: respond to tissue-level signals, divide when told, stop when told, die when told. That last one matters. Programmed cell death, apoptosis, is the biological equivalent of a performance review that ends in termination. The organization removes cells that no longer serve the mission.

A mutation is a fudged report. The cell was supposed to run a DNA damage check before dividing. It skipped it. The cell was supposed to send an honest signal about its internal state. It sent a clean one instead. The cell was supposed to trigger its own termination sequence when the damage was too severe. It didn't.

One fudged report does not bring down a company. The organization has layers of oversight for exactly this reason. The problem is what happens next.

## The cover-up compounds

The employee who fudged one report learns something: nothing happened. No audit. No consequence. So the next report gets fudged too. And the next. Each one is a small, locally rational decision. Why do extra work when nobody checks?

In biology, this is clonal expansion. The cell with the mutation divides. Now there are two cells with the same shortcut. Then four. Then sixteen. Each one filing the same fudged reports: "DNA checks passed. Ready to divide. All clear."

The numbers these cells are faking have real consequences downstream. Other departments make decisions based on those reports. Tissue-level coordination depends on accurate signals from every cell. When one cluster starts lying, the tissue makes bad decisions. Resources get misallocated. Schedules slip. Quality degrades in ways that are hard to trace back to the source.

This is pre-malignant growth. A cluster of cells that look fine on paper but are quietly undermining the organization from within.

## Escalation

Eventually, something trips a wire. Maybe the numbers don't add up in a quarterly review. Maybe a downstream team notices that the inputs they're getting don't match reality. Maybe an auditor spots a pattern.

The problem escalates.

In a company, escalation means the manager's manager gets involved. In your body, it means tissue-level control systems activate. Tumor suppressor pathways like p53 are middle management: they review the cell's internal state and decide whether to halt division, attempt repair, or trigger termination.

This is the first real test. If the suppressor pathway works, the rogue cell gets caught. Repaired or fired. Problem solved. Most pre-cancerous cells die here. Your body catches thousands of these every day.

But sometimes the manager makes a bad call. The repair attempt itself introduces new errors. Error-prone DNA repair is a manager who tries to fix the fraudulent report by rewriting it from memory and gets the numbers wrong in a different way. Now there are two problems: the original fudged data and the botched correction. In biology, this is how repair pathways like non-homologous end joining can introduce new mutations while trying to fix old ones. The intervention made things worse.

And sometimes, when the auditors finally dig in, they discover the problem is bigger than one person. Other employees in the same department were cutting corners too. Maybe independently. Maybe they learned from the first one. Suddenly it's not a personnel issue. It is a culture problem.

This is polyclonal evolution. Multiple independent cell lineages, each with their own mutations, each fudging different reports. The tumor is not one rogue employee. It is a department where the norms have shifted. Much harder to fix than a single bad actor. Removing one doesn't fix the others.

And sometimes, it was an honest mistake to begin with. The employee didn't mean to fudge the report. A copy-paste error. A misread number. But when the manager looked into it, they didn't just miss it. They were already corrupt. The manager had their own reasons to let bad numbers through. Maybe they were covering for their own underperformance. Maybe they had been cutting corners for years.

Now you fire the employee who made the honest mistake. Problem solved? No. The manager is still there. The next employee in that seat inherits a corrupt manager who will let the same problems slide. The fraud continues with a different name on the badge.

This is why killing the tumor cell is not enough when the suppressor pathway is already compromised. A second mutation, this time in p53 itself, means the checkpoint is corrupt. You can eliminate every cancer cell in the tumor, but if the tissue's error-correction system is broken, new rogue cells will emerge and nobody will catch them. The escalation pathway itself is the problem.

Now the problem has to escalate again. To the manager's manager. In biology, this means the immune system. Natural killer cells and cytotoxic T cells are the company's internal affairs division. They don't rely on the local reporting chain. They inspect cells directly, checking surface markers that are hard to fake.

But faking surface markers is exactly what cancer learns to do. PD-L1 expression, MHC downregulation: these are the biological equivalent of an employee who has learned to fool internal affairs. The badge looks right. The credentials check out. But the work isn't getting done.

## Bring in the auditors

Once you know the problem is systemic, you need to understand how deep it goes before you decide what to do about it. You don't fire the whole department based on a hunch. You bring in the auditors.

A biopsy is an audit. You pull records from the department and examine them line by line. What exactly was falsified? Which reports? How far back? A genomic panel, like the MSK-IMPACT test that sequenced my wife's tumor, is a forensic accounting investigation. It doesn't just confirm fraud exists. It tells you which systems were compromised, which signatures were forged, and how the cover-up was structured.

Liquid biopsy, circulating tumor DNA in a blood draw, is the equivalent of monitoring the company's wire transfers without tipping off the department. You can watch for fraudulent transactions in real time without raiding the office. If the numbers change, something is moving.

Imaging, CT scans, PET scans, MRIs, is walking the building and checking which floors are affected. Has the problem stayed in one department? Or has it spread to branch offices? That is staging. Stage 1 is one department. Stage 4 is every office in the company.

The diagnostic workup determines the intervention. You don't send a forensic accountant to fix a problem that requires the FBI. And you don't call the FBI for one employee who fudged a timesheet. The depth of the compromise dictates the response.

## The org chart breaks

Here is the critical point. The problem is not the employee. The problem is how many levels of the org chart they have compromised.

A single rogue employee with a working manager above them is a contained problem. A rogue employee who has corrupted their direct manager is more serious. A rogue employee who has corrupted their manager, fooled internal affairs, and started submitting fraudulent reports to the executive team is a crisis.

This is what we actually measure when we look at tumors. My research shows that the number of distinct organizational systems a tumor has compromised, what I call coupling channels, predicts survival better than the total number of mutations. Across 73,000 patients and three independent datasets.

Think about why. A company can survive an employee who has told a hundred small lies to the same manager. That is a local problem. But a company that has fraud in accounting, compliance, HR, and operations simultaneously? That is a different kind of crisis, even if the total number of lies is smaller.

Mutation count is the total number of lies. Channel count is the number of departments that have been compromised. The second one tells you how sick the company actually is.

## Why bigger companies don't have more fraud

If cancer is employees going rogue, then a bigger company should have more cancer. More employees, more chances for someone to start fudging reports. A company with a million employees should have a thousand times more fraud than a company with a thousand.

This is Peto's paradox, named after the epidemiologist who pointed out that whales don't get more cancer than mice. A blue whale has roughly a thousand times more cells than a human. If cancer were a per-cell coin flip — any cell can go rogue at any time — whales should be riddled with tumors. They aren't. Their cancer rates are comparable to ours, and in some cases lower.

The paradox only exists if you think of cancer as a coin flip. It disappears the moment you think of it as an org chart problem.

A startup with 10 employees has a flat hierarchy. The founder can see every desk. One manager, maybe two. If an employee goes rogue, there's one layer of oversight to catch it. That layer is thin, but the company is small. The probability that *any one employee* goes rogue is low because there aren't many employees. The probability that the one layer of governance catches it is moderate. Net result: small companies occasionally get caught off guard, but the damage is contained because the company is small.

A mid-size company with 10,000 employees has multiple management layers. Team leads, directors, VPs, internal audit, compliance. More employees who *could* go rogue. But proportionally more checkpoints catching them. Each layer of governance reduces the probability that fraud at the level below propagates upward. The fraud doesn't scale linearly with headcount because the governance scales too.

A Fortune 500 with 500,000 employees has the deepest org chart. More layers of review, more redundant audit functions, more independent oversight committees. The absolute number of employees cutting corners might be higher. But the fraction that successfully compromises the organization is lower, because every rogue employee has to evade more layers to cause systemic damage.

This is exactly what we see in biology. Larger organisms have more tumor suppressor mechanisms. More DNA repair pathways. More immune surveillance. More layers of tissue-level coordination. The governance scales with the organization. Not linearly — logarithmically. You don't need a thousand times more management for a thousand times more employees. You need a few more layers. Each layer provides exponential coverage because it monitors the layer below it, not every individual employee.

The data confirms this precisely. Across species, the number of governance layers — distinct tumor suppression mechanisms — scales as the logarithm of cell count. Mice have fewer layers. Humans have more. Whales have more still. The relationship is tight: R² = 0.84. Peto's paradox isn't a paradox. It's the org chart scaling exactly as it should.

And this tells you something about cancer risk that the coin-flip model never could. Cancer susceptibility isn't about how many cells you have. It's about whether your governance is adequate for the size of your organization. A whale with thick governance and a quadrillion cells is safer than a mouse with thin governance and a billion cells. The mouse is the startup with one manager and no audit function. The whale is the Fortune 500 with layers of oversight so deep that a rogue employee in accounting has to corrupt five levels of management before the fraud reaches the executive suite.

The question is never "how many employees do you have?" The question is "how many layers are watching?"

## Different treatments are different kinds of intervention

Once you see cancer as an org chart problem, the treatment landscape starts to make structural sense.

**Surgery is firing the whole department.** Cut the tumor out. Remove every employee in the compromised unit. Works when the problem is contained. Doesn't work when people from that department have already transferred to other offices. That is metastasis.

**Chemotherapy is a company-wide hiring freeze combined with layoffs of anyone who's moving fast.** Kill all rapidly dividing cells. This gets the rogue employees, but it also gets legitimate high-performers: hair follicles, gut lining, bone marrow. A blunt instrument. Works in the short term, but damages the organization's capacity to function. The employees who survive, the slow dividers, the ones with drug efflux pumps, were the hardest to catch in the first place.

**Targeted therapy is sending a specialist to exploit a known vulnerability.** You know the rogue employee is using a specific software backdoor to fake their reports. Patch that exploit. PARP inhibitors work this way: if the cell already has broken DNA repair (like a BRCA or RAD51C mutation), blocking the backup repair pathway forces the cell to die. Precise. Works until the employee finds a different backdoor.

**Immunotherapy is restoring internal affairs.** The rogue employees learned to flash fake badges that fool the company's investigators. Checkpoint inhibitors strip those fake badges. The investigators can see the fraud again. This is why immunotherapy produces durable responses that chemotherapy almost never does. You are not killing the bad actors. You are restoring the organization's ability to police itself. The system is back online, and it adapts as the employees try new tricks.

**Differentiation therapy is the most radical intervention: you convince the rogue employee to start doing their job again.** ATRA in acute promyelocytic leukemia has a greater than 90% cure rate. It doesn't kill the cancer cells. It matures them into functioning neutrophils. The employee who went rogue gets retrained, reassigned, and reintegrated into the org chart. The chain of command is restored at the most fundamental level.

## Why resistance is the default

Every oncologist knows this pattern. Treatment works, then it stops working. The cancer "evolves resistance."

The org chart metaphor explains why. Chemotherapy and targeted therapy impose consequences on a locally rational actor without changing the conditions that made going rogue rational in the first place. The employee is still disconnected from the organization. Still operating in a world where the only information they have is local: what's nearby, what resources are available, what immediate threats exist.

Of course they adapt. You are applying selection pressure to an optimizer that is working correctly within its own constraints. The surviving employees are the ones who found a new way to avoid the audit.

Immunotherapy and differentiation therapy work differently because they change the regime, not just the landscape. They don't ask "how do we catch this employee?" They ask "how do we reconnect this employee to the organization so that going rogue is no longer the rational choice?"

## The bandwidth problem

There is one more piece that the org chart metaphor captures.

Imagine the CEO is already overwhelmed. Five simultaneous crises. Can't sleep. Can't focus. Every meeting is an interruption that displaces the last one. The company's leadership has no spare bandwidth.

Now add the rogue employee to that situation. How quickly does the problem get caught? How effectively does the organization respond?

This is what happens when cancer co-occurs with conditions that tax the body's regulatory capacity. Depression, anxiety, ADHD, chronic stress: states where the organism's "management bandwidth," its bounded context, is already consumed. The signaling systems that would normally catch a rogue cell are busy. The immune surveillance that would normally escalate the problem is degraded. Not because the hardware is broken. Because the system is thrashing, spending its limited attention on too many things at once.

This is not metaphor. The molecular mechanism is specific: neural computation requires DNA double-strand breaks in chromatin remodeling genes. The same genes that cancer needs to corrupt. When the CEO works 18-hour days on five crises, they are wearing out the same infrastructure that the rogue employee needs to compromise. Treating the CEO's burnout is not a luxury. It is a security measure.

This predicts that treating depression, anxiety, and ADHD in cancer patients should improve cancer outcomes directly. Not as palliative care. As a first-class co-treatment. Reduce the load on the org chart so it can deal with the actual threat.

## What this means

Cancer is not a disease of broken cells. It is a disease of broken relationships between cells and the organizations they belong to. The cell is not malfunctioning. It is functioning correctly in a world that has gotten very small.

The org chart metaphor is not decoration. It is the actual structure. Multicellular life is a hierarchy of bounded agents coordinating through escalation. Cancer is what happens when that coordination breaks. How many levels break determines how bad it gets. And treatment works best when it restores the chain rather than just punishing the defector.

Every company knows that you can't solve a culture problem by firing individuals. You have to fix the systems, the communication channels, the reporting structures, the feedback loops that keep people connected to the mission.

Cancer is the same problem. Just written in a different language.

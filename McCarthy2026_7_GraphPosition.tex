\title{Mutation Position in the Signaling Graph Predicts\\
Cancer Survival Beyond Channel Count}
\author{Patrick D.\ McCarthy}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Paper~6 of this series showed that the number of coupling channels
disrupted by a tumor's mutations predicts overall survival. This paper
asks whether position \emph{within} a channel matters. We classify
channel genes as hubs (high-connectivity, upstream regulators with many
downstream targets) or leaves (downstream, specific effectors) and test
whether hub mutations carry worse prognosis than leaf mutations in the
same channel.

Across two independent MSK-IMPACT datasets totaling 54{,}492 patients
with survival data, hub mutations predict significantly worse survival
than leaf mutations. Among single-channel patients, hub-only median
overall survival is 16.5--18.0 months versus 20.2--21.4 months for
leaf-only (log-rank $p < 10^{-11}$ in both datasets). In multivariate
Cox regression controlling for channel count, hub status carries
HR\,$=$\,1.48--1.54 ($p < 10^{-20}$). The effect replicates
within PI3K/Growth (HR\,$\approx$\,1.30) and Cell Cycle
(HR\,$\approx$\,1.37--1.47) but not within DDR ($p = 0.67$),
consistent with DDR's greater pathway redundancy.

A case study of BRCA1 (hub) versus BRCA2 (leaf) within the DDR channel
shows divergent phenotypes: BRCA1-mutant breast cancers are enriched
for triple-negative subtype (34.1\% vs.\ 19.4\%) and pleural
metastasis (34.1\% vs.\ 14.9\%, $p = 0.031$), while BRCA2-mutant
breast cancers show higher bone metastasis (67.2\% vs.\ 56.1\%).
RAD51C-mutant breast patients (all 10 HR+, zero triple-negative)
phenocopy BRCA2, not BRCA1.

Metastatic tropism analysis reveals that endocrine channel mutations
predict \emph{more} bone metastasis, not less---because ESR1 and AR
mutations are gain-of-function resistance events, not loss-of-function
severance. The coupling-channel framework must therefore distinguish
severance (LOF) from resistance (GOF). Cell Cycle mutations (TP53,
RB1) are the strongest positive predictor of metastasis to every
organ site. Immune channel mutations reduce detectable distant
metastasis across bone, liver, and lung.
\end{abstract}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Paper~6 \cite{McCarthy2026f} established that coupling-channel count
predicts cancer survival better than mutation count across 73{,}593
patients. The number of independent organizational systems a tumor
escapes determines prognosis. That analysis explicitly noted a
limitation: it did not distinguish hub from leaf mutations within a
channel.

This paper addresses that limitation. Within a single coupling channel,
not all genes occupy the same position in the signaling graph. Some
genes sit at high-connectivity upstream positions---they regulate many
downstream targets and integrate multiple inputs. Others sit at
downstream, terminal positions---specific effectors with narrow
functional scope.

The question is whether graph position carries prognostic information
beyond channel count. If it does, then the coupling-channel framework
has a second axis of resolution: not just \emph{how many} channels are
severed, but \emph{where} in the signaling graph the severance occurs.

We also examine two downstream predictions. First, BRCA1 and BRCA2
both belong to the DDR channel but occupy different graph positions.
If position matters, they should produce different disease phenotypes
despite sharing a channel. Second, metastatic tropism should depend on
which channels are severed and on the functional consequence of the
mutation---severance versus resistance.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Datasets.}
We use the same three clinical genomic datasets as Paper~6
\cite{McCarthy2026e}: MSK-IMPACT 2017 \cite{Zehir2017}, MSK-MET 2021
(MetTropism) \cite{Nguyen2022}, and MSK-IMPACT 50K
\cite{Bandlamudi2024}. The same mutation filters apply: non-silent
somatic variants mapped to six coupling channels (DDR, Cell Cycle,
PI3K/Growth, Endocrine, Immune, Tissue Architecture).

\textbf{Hub/leaf classification.}
We classified channel genes into hub and leaf positions based on known
pathway topology. Hub genes are high-connectivity upstream regulators
with many downstream targets. Leaf genes are downstream specific
effectors with narrow functional scope.

The classification is as follows:

\emph{Hub genes.}
DDR: ATM, ATR, BRCA1.
Cell Cycle: TP53, RB1, MYC.
PI3K/Growth: KRAS, PTEN, PIK3CA, EGFR, NF1.
Endocrine: ESR1, AR.
Immune: B2M, JAK1, JAK2.
Tissue Architecture: APC, CDH1, SMAD4.

\emph{Leaf genes.}
DDR: BRCA2, RAD51C, RAD51D, CHEK2, PALB2.
Cell Cycle: CDK4, CDK6, CDKN2A, MDM2.
PI3K/Growth: AKT1, BRAF, MTOR, ERBB2, FGFR1, FGFR2, FGFR3.
Endocrine: FOXA1, GATA3.
Immune: HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, STAT1.
Tissue Architecture: CTNNB1, AXIN1, NOTCH1, FBXW7.

\textbf{Patient classification.}
For each patient, we determined whether their mutations fell
exclusively in hub genes, exclusively in leaf genes, or in both.
For the multivariate analysis, we defined a binary variable
\texttt{hub\_any} indicating whether the patient carried at least one
hub mutation in any channel.

\textbf{Statistical analysis.}
Kaplan--Meier curves and log-rank tests compare hub-only versus
leaf-only patients. Cox proportional hazards models estimate hazard
ratios (HR) with 95\% confidence intervals. The multivariate model
includes both \texttt{hub\_any} and \texttt{channel\_count} to test
whether graph position adds prognostic information beyond channel
count.

For the BRCA comparison, we use Fisher exact tests for categorical
comparisons (breast subtype, co-mutation rates, metastatic site
frequencies). For tropism analysis, we use chi-squared tests and
cancer-type-adjusted logistic regression with six channel-severed
indicators as predictors.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Hub mutations predict worse survival than leaf mutations}
\label{sec:hub-leaf}

Among single-channel patients in MSK-IMPACT 50K, hub-only patients
($n = 10{,}207$) had median overall survival of 16.5 months versus
20.2 months for leaf-only patients ($n = 2{,}710$; log-rank
$p = 6.5 \times 10^{-22}$). Cox regression: HR\,$=$\,1.24 (95\% CI
1.14--1.35, $p = 3.3 \times 10^{-7}$). The MetTropism dataset
replicated this: hub-only median OS 18.0 months versus leaf-only
21.4 months ($n = 5{,}833$ vs.\ $1{,}517$; log-rank
$p = 2.2 \times 10^{-11}$).

Table~\ref{tab:within-channel} shows within-channel results.
PI3K/Growth and Cell Cycle both show significant hub--leaf differences.
DDR does not.

HR\,$>$\,1 indicates worse survival for hub mutations.}

\midrule

The DDR null result is notable. ATM, ATR, and BRCA1 (hubs) show no
survival difference relative to BRCA2, RAD51C, CHEK2, and PALB2
(leaves). This is consistent with DDR's architecture: the pathway has
substantial redundancy, and loss of a hub gene (e.g., ATM) can be
partially compensated by parallel repair pathways. In PI3K/Growth and
Cell Cycle, hub genes (KRAS, TP53) sit at bottleneck positions with
less functional redundancy.

\textbf{Multivariate model: hub status beyond channel count.}
In the full MSK-IMPACT 50K cohort ($n = 34{,}439$), a multivariate
Cox model with both \texttt{hub\_any} and \texttt{channel\_count}
yields: hub\_any HR\,$=$\,1.54 (95\% CI 1.44--1.64,
$p = 1.7 \times 10^{-38}$); channel\_count HR\,$=$\,0.94 (95\% CI
0.93--0.96, $p = 2.5 \times 10^{-13}$). The MetTropism dataset
replicates: hub\_any HR\,$=$\,1.48 (95\% CI 1.36--1.61,
$p = 1.8 \times 10^{-20}$); channel\_count HR\,$=$\,0.96 (95\% CI
0.94--0.98, $p = 6.2 \times 10^{-4}$).

Graph position adds prognostic information beyond channel count.
Hub mutations are independently associated with worse survival after
controlling for the number of channels disrupted. The protective
direction of channel\_count, conditional on hub status, replicates the
finding from Paper~6 \cite{McCarthy2026f}: more mutations in the same
channel (which raises channel count's denominator relative to its
numerator) indicates deeper commitment to one escape route.

\subsection{Case study: BRCA1 vs.\ BRCA2}
\label{sec:brca}

BRCA1 and BRCA2 both belong to the DDR channel. BRCA1 is a hub:
it integrates DNA damage signals, participates in cell cycle
checkpoint activation, and interacts with multiple repair complexes.
BRCA2 is a leaf: it loads RAD51 onto single-stranded DNA during
homologous recombination, a specific effector function.

Despite sharing a channel, they produce different disease phenotypes.

\textbf{Breast cancer subtype.}
Among MSK-MET breast cancer patients, BRCA1-mutant tumors show
34.1\% triple-negative subtype versus 19.4\% for BRCA2
($n = 41$ vs.\ $67$). BRCA2-mutant tumors are predominantly
HR+ (50.7\% ductal HR+/HER2$-$ plus 20.9\% lobular HR+).

RAD51C is informative. Among 10 RAD51C-mutant breast patients
in MSK-MET, all are HR+ (60.0\% ductal HR+/HER2$-$, 40.0\%
lobular HR+). Zero are triple-negative. RAD51C phenocopies BRCA2,
not BRCA1---consistent with its leaf position in the DDR graph
(RAD51C is a direct RAD51 paralog, functionally downstream).

\textbf{Co-mutation patterns.}
BRCA1-mutant patients show higher TP53 co-mutation than BRCA2:
58.7\% versus 52.1\% (Fisher $p = 0.00045$). This is consistent
with BRCA1's hub position. BRCA1 loss destabilizes cell cycle
checkpoint control (BRCA1 interacts directly with TP53 pathway
components), creating selective pressure for TP53 inactivation.
BRCA2 loss, being a more specific repair defect, generates less
checkpoint pressure.

\textbf{Metastatic tropism.}
Table~\ref{tab:brca-tropism} shows metastatic site frequencies
for BRCA1 vs.\ BRCA2 in breast cancer patients.

The significant finding is pleural metastasis: 34.1\% for BRCA1
versus 14.9\% for BRCA2 ($p = 0.031$). BRCA2-mutant tumors trend
toward higher bone metastasis (67.2\% vs.\ 56.1\%), consistent with
their HR+ predominance and intact endocrine coupling. RAD51C again
tracks with BRCA2: high bone (70.0\%), low pleura (20.0\%).

Small sample sizes limit significance on several comparisons.
The pan-cancer analysis ($n = 462$ BRCA1 vs.\ $n = 872$ BRCA2)
confirms the pleural finding ($p = 0.0005$) and shows a trend
toward higher CNS/brain metastasis for BRCA1 (15.2\% vs.\ 11.8\%,
$p = 0.087$).

\label{sec:tropism}

preference using cancer-type-adjusted logistic regression across
25{,}775 MSK-MET patients. Table~\ref{tab:tropism} shows odds ratios

Three findings stand out.

\textbf{Cell Cycle mutations predict general metastatic aggressiveness.}
TP53 and RB1 mutations are the strongest positive predictor of
metastasis to \emph{every} organ site tested: bone (OR\,$=$\,1.30),
liver (1.39), lung (1.12), brain (1.48), adrenal (1.43), all
$p < 10^{-4}$. This is not tropism. It is a general aggressiveness
signal. Cell cycle checkpoint loss increases metastatic capacity
across the board.

\textbf{Immune channel mutations reduce detectable distant metastasis.}
Immune-severed tumors (B2M, JAK1/2, HLA loss) show significantly
\emph{lower} metastasis rates to bone (OR\,$=$\,0.69,
$p = 10^{-8}$), liver (OR\,$=$\,0.56, $p = 5 \times 10^{-18}$),
and lung (OR\,$=$\,0.69, $p = 7 \times 10^{-9}$). Immune evasion
mutations may reduce detectable distant disease---either because these
tumors are controlled locally by residual immune function, or because
immune-evaded tumors establish micrometastases that are clinically
occult at the time of sequencing.

\textbf{Endocrine channel mutations predict more bone metastasis,
not less.}
This is the critical finding for framework refinement. The naive
prediction from the coupling-channel model is that endocrine channel
severance should reduce bone tropism, because the endocrine niche
(hormone-dependent bone microenvironment) should become inaccessible
to a tumor that has lost endocrine coupling. The data show the
opposite: endocrine-mutant tumors have \emph{higher} bone metastasis
(OR\,$=$\,1.14, $p = 0.007$).

The explanation is that most endocrine channel mutations are not
loss-of-function severance events. They are gain-of-function
resistance mutations. ESR1 ligand-binding domain mutations (Y537S,
D538G) constitutively activate the estrogen receptor under aromatase
inhibitor pressure. AR amplification and point mutations in
castration-resistant prostate cancer maintain androgen signaling
despite androgen deprivation therapy.

The data confirm this at the single-gene level. In prostate cancer,
AR-mutant patients show 76.7\% bone metastasis versus 51.4\% for
AR-wildtype ($p < 10^{-5}$, $n = 90$ vs.\ $2{,}082$). In breast
cancer, ESR1/GATA3/FOXA1-mutant patients show 57.7\% bone metastasis
versus 46.2\% for wildtype ($p < 10^{-6}$, $n = 617$ vs.\
$1{,}992$).

A gain-of-function mutation in a coupling channel gene is not
severance. It is the tumor maintaining that coupling under
therapeutic pressure. The bone tropism data validate this
distinction: GOF endocrine mutations maintain endocrine coupling,
so bone tropism (an endocrine-dependent niche) persists and even
intensifies.

The coupling-channel framework must therefore distinguish two
classes of mutation: severance (LOF, which removes coupling and
restricts the tumor's niche) and resistance (GOF, which maintains
coupling under pressure and preserves niche access).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Graph position adds a second axis of prognostic information beyond
channel count. A tumor with a hub mutation in PI3K/Growth (e.g., KRAS)
has worse prognosis than a tumor with a leaf mutation in the same
channel (e.g., BRAF), even after controlling for the number of
channels disrupted. The effect is large: HR\,$=$\,1.48--1.54 for
hub status in the multivariate model.

The DDR exception is informative. The DDR channel shows no hub--leaf
survival difference, unlike PI3K/Growth and Cell Cycle. This is
consistent with DDR's pathway architecture. DDR has multiple
parallel repair mechanisms (homologous recombination, non-homologous
end joining, mismatch repair, base excision repair). Loss of a hub
gene like ATM can be partially compensated by alternative repair
pathways. In contrast, TP53 and KRAS sit at true bottleneck
positions in their respective networks. The hub--leaf distinction
is not universal; it depends on the degree of pathway redundancy.

The BRCA1/BRCA2 case study is the clearest illustration. Same
channel, different graph position, different disease. BRCA1 (hub)
produces more triple-negative breast cancer, more TP53 co-mutation,
and more pleural metastasis. BRCA2 (leaf) produces more HR+ disease
and trends toward more bone metastasis. RAD51C (leaf, functionally
downstream of BRCA2) phenocopies BRCA2 across all measured
endpoints. These phenotypic differences are predicted by graph
position and are not predicted by channel membership alone.

The GOF/LOF distinction is the most important refinement to emerge
from this analysis. The original coupling-channel framework treats
any mutation in a channel gene as a potential severance event. The
tropism data show this is wrong for endocrine channel genes. ESR1
and AR mutations in the metastatic setting are overwhelmingly
gain-of-function resistance mutations that \emph{maintain} endocrine
coupling. The framework must distinguish severance from resistance.

For trial design, the implications are direct. Stratifying patients
by graph position within a pathway---not just by pathway
membership---should improve prognostic accuracy. A KRAS-mutant
patient and a BRAF-mutant patient are not equivalent even though
both carry PI3K/Growth channel mutations. A BRCA1-mutant patient
and a BRCA2-mutant patient are not equivalent even though both
carry DDR channel mutations. Current basket trial designs that group
patients by pathway may be grouping biologically distinct populations.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Mutation position in the signaling graph predicts cancer survival
beyond channel count. Hub mutations carry worse prognosis than leaf
mutations within the same channel, with HR\,$=$\,1.48--1.54 after
controlling for channel count. The effect replicates in PI3K/Growth
and Cell Cycle but not DDR, consistent with differential pathway
redundancy.

The BRCA1/BRCA2 case study shows that graph position predicts
phenotype within a channel. The metastatic tropism analysis shows
that the coupling-channel framework must distinguish severance from
resistance: gain-of-function mutations maintain coupling and preserve
niche-specific tropism.

Graph position is the second axis. Channel count tells you how many
organizational systems the tumor has escaped. Graph position tells
you how completely it has escaped each one.
